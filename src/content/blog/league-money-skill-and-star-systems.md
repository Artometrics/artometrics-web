<main class="art-article-main">
<p class="art-p">A sports league is a measurement system before it is an entertainment product. It decides how much money can matter, how much one player can matter, how often fans get to reset emotionally, and how widely the story can travel.</p>
<p class="art-p">This report builds a cross-league lens for future Artometrics profiles: money, skill, star leverage, schedule density, and culture are not interchangeable across sports.</p>

<h2 id="dataset-context" class="anchored">DATASET CONTEXT</h2>
<p class="art-p">The figures combine public valuation summaries, reference-record league structures, and editorial indices. The indices are deliberately transparent: they exist to compare systems, not to pretend every input is observed with laboratory precision.</p>
<p class="art-p">A commissioner, analyst, or fan would ask different questions. The useful layer is the shared one: what does the league make easy, what does it make scarce, and what does it make emotionally expensive?</p>

<p class="art-p">The NFL does not need every team to be glamorous. Its media structure makes the ordinary franchise expensive because the league product is scarce, synchronized, and nationally distributed.</p>
<p class="art-p">That is a metric difference and a cultural difference: football teams are local flags plugged into a national machine.</p>
<h2 id="star-leverage" class="anchored">CHART 2 - STAR LEVERAGE</h2>
<figure class="art-chart">
  <div class="art-chart-live" data-chart="/data/articles/league-money-skill-and-star-systems/charts/chart2_star_leverage.plotly.json" data-source="Data: Forbes, Spotrac, Basketball Reference, Baseball Reference, Pro Football Reference, Hockey Reference - ARTOMETRICS" role="img" aria-label="The NBA gives individual stars the strongest control over team identity"></div>
  <figcaption class="art-chart-caption">The NBA gives individual stars the strongest control over team identity</figcaption>

<p class="art-p">A basketball star touches a huge share of possessions. A baseball star can disappear into lineup order and variance. A football star may be trapped inside scheme, injuries, and roster scale.</p>
<p class="art-p">This is why NBA identity travels through names, while NFL identity often travels through systems.</p>

<p class="art-p">Salary caps, luxury taxes, revenue sharing, and roster rules are not administrative details. They are identity engines.</p>
<p class="art-p">The MLB gap is partly the point: baseball lets market and development systems express themselves with less hard compression than the NFL or NHL.</p>

<p class="art-p">A baseball team can lose four straight and still be fine. An NFL team can lose two games and trigger a referendum. The schedule is a psychology machine.</p>
<p class="art-p">This matters for future reports because pain, momentum, and panic are not measured on the same clock across leagues.</p>
<h2 id="belonging" class="anchored">CHART 5 - BELONGING</h2>
<figure class="art-chart">

<p class="art-p">The Premier League and NBA travel globally with exceptional ease. The NFL remains more domestically ritualized even as its value dominates.</p>
<p class="art-p">The Artometrics question is not simply which league is bigger. It is what kind of belonging each league manufactures.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion" class="anchored">CONCLUSION</h2>
<p class="art-p">The cleanest conclusion is that league design creates fan reality. The same owner, player, or city would produce a different identity under a different schedule, cap structure, roster size, and media architecture.</p>
<p class="art-p">That is why future team profiles should not compare teams without comparing the sports that made them.</p>
