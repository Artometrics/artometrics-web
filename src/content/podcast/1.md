**Interviewer:** Welcome to the Artometrics podcast. Juliet, you edit the Culture desk. What does it mean to run a data desk inside a magazine about creative industries?

**Juliet Ramos:** It means refusing the split between "numbers people" and "culture people." Anime rankings, Billboard charts, Steam libraries, LEGO themes — these are already datasets. The creative economy generates more measurable behavior every year. Artometrics exists because those records deserve the same editorial rigor as a financial newsroom, but aimed at readers who care about art, place, and power.

---

**Interviewer:** Where do your reports usually start?

**Juliet Ramos:** Often with a public dataset someone else maintained — TidyTuesday releases, government tables, platform exports, MusicBrainz, MyAnimeList aggregates. The question is never "can we chart this?" It is "what claim would help a curious reader understand the system?" A chart should teach one useful thing, with the source path visible.

---

**Interviewer:** What do you want listeners to take from the Culture desk?

**Juliet Ramos:** That enthusiasm and evidence reinforce each other. You can love a franchise and still ask how its revenue compounds. You can be new to data journalism and still follow a report if we disclose assumptions, label editorial indices honestly, and write like adults. That standard is what makes Artometrics a magazine — not a dashboard.

---

**Interviewer:** What's coming next on the desk?

**Juliet Ramos:** More reports where platform behavior meets institutional history — awards prestige, streaming engagement, export identity. If the creative industry leaves a public trail, we want to read it carefully and publish the limits alongside the finding.
